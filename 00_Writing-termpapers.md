## **Structure of your term paper** 

- **Titlepage:** Title, your name, matriculation number, e-mail, my name, course 

- **(Table of contents)** 

- **Introduction:** Topic, motivation, RESEARCH QUESTION, brief summary of data and method, evt brief outline 

- **Theoretical background:** Theoretical framework and concepts directly relevant to your RQ. All the concepts that you will apply to analyse and interpret your data. No sequence of summaries of studies! 

- **Data and Methodology:** Rule of thumb: needs to be detailed enough that some else can follow your explanations and arrive at the same results. Illustrate your methodological steps by presenting and discussing (difficult/unclear) examples 

- **Results and analysis:** Should include specific examples and interpretations of these examples. Note: linguistic examples are enumerated throughout the paper (see a research article for how to do this). 

- **Conclusion:** Brief summary, overall conclusion, limitations, outlook: what else could/should one investigate? Make sure to link your study to the wider research context (theoretical background, motivation) 

- **(Data):** If you include corpus data or other “primary sources”, you list their references in a “Data” section, before the list of references 

- **References:** Note the conventions for English linguistics!

## **Formatting in English linguistics** 

- Use Author-Year citation format! 

   - In text referencing: 

As shown by previous research, laughter can be used as a starting point to study humour (Miller **2021** ). Irony is defined as […] in the influential study by Smith ( **1999:** 34). 

- Note: Don’t use “ibid.”, but repeat year of publication each time 

   - In linguistics, always include the year of publication with in-text references: 

      - (Smith 2021) 

      - (Smith 2021: 234) 

      - As Smith already stated, “this is an excellent example” (2021: 234). 

- Reference list: 

Dynel, Marta. **2016.** With or without intentions: Accountability and (un)intentional humour in film talk. _Journal of Pragmatics_ 95. 67–78. Dynel, Marta. **2018.** _Irony, Deception and Humour. Seeking the truth about overt and covert untruthfulness_ . Boston/Berlin: De Gruyter Mouton. 

- Use hanging indent (first line is further to the left than rest of an entry). Use the paragraph formatting options of your word processor software to achieve this. 

- Make sure that you follow linguistic conventions for formatting your list of references. The year of publication follows directly after the author name(s). 

- Use full names of authors. 

- Inform yourself about the formatting of different types of publications (books, journal articles, book contributions, etc.) and make sure that you use complete and correctly formatted information. You can find examples in the Studienführer and in the list of references of your course programme.

## **Referencing your sources** 

**For each single sentence in your paper it must be absolutely clear whether it represents:** – Your own ideas in your own words 

   - -> no reference 

- Someone else’s ideas/information in your own words 

   - -> reference, no quotation marks 

- Someone else’s ideas/information in that person’s words -> direct quote, reference

## **Other serious problems with improper referencing** 

- **Presenting quotes out of context** 

   - Make sure that you properly read the literature that you reference and that the claims that you attribute to the authors represent their claims and ideas 

      - **Making unsupported claims:** Claims that are not backed up by relevant references and/or data that you present 

- **Listing in-text references that are not relevant to the claims made** 

   - See above 

- **Quotation patchwork:** 

   - Don’t include sections or paragraphs that consist mainly of direct quote fragments, sewn together with a few phrases from you 

   - Instead: Develop your own argumentation, in your own words, while attributing ideas by other researchers appropriately

## Watch out for “Germanisms”, e.g.: 

- “In **the following,** I will show that” 

   - ➜ “In what follows, …” 

   - ➜ “In the following chapter/section, …” 

   - ➜ Even better: 

      - “In this paper, …” 

      - Just skip 

Watch out for sentence connectors that are at odds with the logic of your text (e.g. _therefore, despite this, nevertheless)_
