## **Term paper writing** 

## _**1. Formal aspects and plagiarism**_ 

Before you start, you should consult the _Studienführer_ (available on the website of the _Anglistisches Seminar_ ). There, you can find general information on term paper writing, including inforation on how to cite correctly. 

I advise you to read the chapter by Wray and Bloomer (2006) on plagiarism. The book is available at the Department library, I’m happy to make the chapter available to you if you need it. Please note that any form of plagiarism means you fail your assignment. 

Make sure that you declare all your sources of information, no matter whether you quote word by word or whether you paraphrase. For each sentence it needs to be entirely clear whether it a) presents your own ideas in your own words (no reference); b) someone else’s ideas in your own words (indirect quotation); or c) someone else’s ideas in their words (direct quotation). Also make sure that all your quotations are 100% accurate. 

## _**2. Topic Search**_ 

If you write a term paper in the context of a seminar, you will already have plenty of starting points for your topic. If not (for instance if you write your final thesis), I suggest that you proceed in the following manner: 

1. Look through Wray and Bloomer (2006) for general ideas of research areas 

2. Find an introductory textbook to the research area you are interested in and read this 

3. Look at handbooks on your topic to find out how the field is structured 

4. Search relevant research literature on your topic. Make sure that you use the MLA database to search for relevant literature and that you do not only search for books (monographs) but also for journal articles. Much relevant research is published in this form. 

## _**3. Structure**_ 

This section presents some recommendations for structuring your paper (for further information, see also the _Studienführer_ ). Note that **the names of the sections do not have to be identical to the labels below** (e.g. it would be a good idea to name the section in which you present your theoretical approach according to the topics you discuss, rather than “theoretical part”). For a general model of your term paper, look at linguistic research articles (articles published in linguistic journals, which present original research). Your paper should more or less follow the structure of such articles. This also gives you an idea of how much detail is needed in theoretical sections (which should not be too general, see below), and how linguistic data is presented in examples, tables and charts (e.g. the use of captions and enumeration).

Start with a motivation of your research question (What do we want to know that we do not know yet? Why is this interesting?). For instance, you can present an example that illustrates the phenomenon you are interested in, or you can start by presenting everyday life observations relevant to your research question). - **Theoretical part:** Here you introduce the theoretical background that is necessary for your research project. In particular, you have to introduce all the theoretical concepts that you rely on, complete with definitions and illustrative examples (preferably from your own data). The theoretical concepts and models need to be presented in the context of previous research on your topic. You should briefly discuss alternative approaches to the one you chose and describe the motivation behind your decision. Like for all the other sections, your research question needs to be at the centre of your text. In other words, **DO NOT just summarise previous research in your topic area;** instead, make it clear for each individual point how it is relevant for your research question. Also, **DO NOT provide a summary of the history of the research field in question.** Just focus on the theoretical concepts that you rely on in your analysis. 

- **Data and Methodology** (can be combined into one section or split up in two): Here you describe the data you used and the way in which you analysed it. It is important that you describe the methods you used for your data analysis in detail. After reading this section, one should be able to exactly replicate your analysis and arrive at the same results. If this is not the case, your text is not detailed enough. If you carry out quantitative analyses, you should include a discussion on points such as the reliability of your categorisations and/or the precision and recall of your corpus queries, where applicable. You should also discuss the advantages and limitations of your data and methodology. 

- **Results and Analysis:** Here you present and analyse your findings. Provide an overview of your results first, e.g. in the form of a table or a graph. Then discuss your results in detail and illustrate them with examples from your data. Note that all examples need to be formatted as linguistic examples and enumerated throughout your paper. Don’t forget to include a discussion and interpretation of each example you present. Compare your results to those given in the literature. 

- **Conclusion:** Summarise your main findings and draw a conclusion based on these findings. Go back to your research question(s)/hypothesis and say whether you could answer the question and whether you found supporting evidence for your hypothesis. You should also indicate possible limitations of your results and point out aspects that demand further study. 

- **References** : The list of references includes all the sources you have used in your paper (and only those). Consult the _Studienführer_ on further details Note that the conventions for linguistics are different from those in literature!
